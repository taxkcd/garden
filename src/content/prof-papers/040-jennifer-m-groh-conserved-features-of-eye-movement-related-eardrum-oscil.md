# 040 · Conserved features of eye movement related eardrum oscillations (EMREOs) across humans and monkeys

## At a glance

- **Professor:** Jennifer M. Groh
- **Institution:** Duke University
- **Paper:** [Conserved features of eye movement related eardrum oscillations (EMREOs) across humans and monkeys](https://europepmc.org/articles/PMC10404921?pdf=render)
- **Authors:** Stephanie N. Lovich, Cynthia D. King, David L. K. Murphy, Hossein Abbasi, Patrick Bruns, Christopher A. Shera, Jennifer M. Groh
- **Year:** 2023

## Paper overview

This study investigates how the eardrum moves in response to eye movements in both humans and rhesus monkeys. These eye movement-related eardrum oscillations (EMREOs) may help the brain align auditory and visual spatial information, which are normally processed in different coordinate systems. The research shows that EMREOs depend mainly on horizontal eye displacement and are conserved across species, although some differences exist in waveform frequency and individual variability.

### Why it matters

**Research problem:** Auditory and visual spatial information are encoded in different coordinate systems—auditory cues are head-centered, while visual cues are eye-centered. The brain must reconcile these to integrate sensory information accurately. The recently discovered EMREOs suggest that this reconciliation might begin at the auditory periphery, but how EMREOs contribute to this process and their conserved features across species remain poorly understood.

**Why it matters:** Understanding how the brain integrates auditory and visual spatial information is critical for comprehending multisensory perception and spatial localization. Insights into EMREOs could reveal early mechanisms of sensory integration and inform how eye movements influence auditory processing, with implications for neuroscience and auditory health.

**Key contributions:**

- Demonstrated that EMREOs depend strongly and consistently on horizontal eye displacement in both humans and monkeys.
- Showed that EMREO signals are similar across different behavioral tasks (visually guided and free-viewing saccades).
- Provided a detailed cross-species comparison revealing conserved and differing features of EMREOs.
- Introduced a regression analysis framework to quantify EMREO dependence on eye movement parameters.
- Identified that waveform frequency of EMREOs is higher in monkeys than humans.
