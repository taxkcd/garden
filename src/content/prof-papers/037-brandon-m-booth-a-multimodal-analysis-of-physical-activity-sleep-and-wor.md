# 037 · A multimodal analysis of physical activity, sleep, and work shift in nurses with wearable sensor data

## At a glance

- **Professor:** Brandon M. Booth
- **Institution:** University of Memphis
- **Paper:** [A multimodal analysis of physical activity, sleep, and work shift in nurses with wearable sensor data](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-87029-w)
- **Authors:** Tiantian Feng, Brandon M. Booth, Brooke Baldwin-Rodríguez, Felipe Osorno, Shrikanth Narayanan
- **Year:** 2021

## Paper overview

This study used commercial wearable sensors to monitor physical activity, sleep, and circadian rhythms in 113 nurses working day and night shifts over 10 weeks. It found that night shift nurses are more sedentary, have poorer sleep quality, and report lower life satisfaction compared to day shift nurses. Wearable sensors effectively captured these behavioral and physiological differences, providing objective insights into the impacts of shift work on nurse well-being.

### Why it matters

**Research problem:** Night shift work disrupts circadian rhythms and is associated with negative health outcomes, physical inactivity, poor sleep quality, and reduced life satisfaction among nurses. However, prior studies often rely on subjective surveys and short durations, lacking objective, long-term data on these effects.

**Why it matters:** Understanding the physiological and behavioral impacts of shift work on nurses is critical to improving their well-being, job satisfaction, and performance, which ultimately affects healthcare quality and patient safety.

**Key contributions:**

- Provided a large-scale, long-duration (10 weeks) multimodal dataset combining wearable sensor data and self-reports from 113 nurses.
- Quantified differences in physical activity, sleep patterns, and circadian misalignment between day and night shift nurses.
- Identified associations between wearable sensor data and behavioral self-reports specific to shift schedules.
